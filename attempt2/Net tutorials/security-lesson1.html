Lesson 1: How to Protect Yourself on the Internet

Understanding Phishing Links

Phishing links are used by scammers and attackers to trick or deceive people into revealing their personal information by disguising those links as being from reliable sources.

These types of links are usually seen through:

  • Emails
  • Social media
  • QR codes
  • Text messages
  • Attachments
  • Fake ads

Common types include Spear Phishing, HTTPS Phishing, and Clone Phishing.

How to Recognize Phishing Links

  • The link is not secure (doesn't start with https://)
  • The website is designed poorly and contains grammatical flaws
  • The website contains many pop-ups
  • The website requests unusual forms of payment (like Bitcoin)
  • The link is attempting to emulate a popular website

Phishing Scams in Emails

Phishing links often appear in emails that seem to be from real companies but are fake. Warning signs include:

  • Grammatical or spelling errors
  • Weird email addresses in the "From" field
  • Links that go to fake websites controlled by attackers

How to Recognize Phishing Links in Web Links

Look for the Lock

Check for HTTPS and a lock icon in the address bar. HTTP without the "S" means your data isn't secure.

Look for Misspellings

Phishers use URLs like paypa1.com instead of paypal.com

Verify Domain Names

apple.com.secure-login.com is not the same as apple.com

How to Protect Your Information

Enable Multi-Factor Authentication (MFA)

Requires multiple verification steps:

  • Knowledge: Something you know (password)
  • Possession: Something you have (phone)
  • Inherence: Something you are (fingerprint)

Additional Protection Tips

  • Never enter credentials after clicking links in emails
  • Use a password manager to generate and store strong passwords
  • Keep your software and browsers updated
  • Be skeptical of urgent requests for personal information
